Orthodoxy
By G.K. Chesterton

Part I - Introduction

The object of the following pages is a very plain one. I have attempted in them to describe my own philosophy of life, not by the old method of equipping the reader with a sort of previous bill of materials to be used in the construction, and then beginning to build up again from the lowest foundations—but by a much more difficult and generally deprecated method, of beginning with the human and personal, and only insisting on the Cosmic and Celestial by way of conclusion.

In a word, I wish not to expound Catholicism, but to expound myself as a Catholic. Now this might be misunderstood. By "myself" I do not mean my subjective sensations, which are quite likely to be abnormal or diseased. I mean merely the average man's view of life, so far as I, an average man, have succeeded in achieving it.

The man who makes the usual experiments in the usual manner will be made to understand the usual conclusions. But the very fact of the matter is that life is not a chain of logical conclusions. Life is a story. It is a narrative of adventure. It is not a deduction from principles; it is a series of surprising incidents.

I have often had a floating fancy that something extremely modern might be quite extremely medieval, and that something very new might be very ancient.

This idea has come to me in connection with all things; but in connection with this religion particularly. Christianity has not so much changed the human heart; as it has shown human nature in its true light.
